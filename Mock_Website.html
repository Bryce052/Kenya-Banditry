Methodology

Data Collection

Our database systematically tracks banditry incidents reported in Kenyan media sources. We employ a multi-source approach to ensure comprehensive coverage:

  • Daily monitoring of national and regional newspapers
  • Online news platforms and digital media outlets
  • Radio broadcasts in affected regions
  • Official government reports and press releases
  • NGO and civil society documentation

Incident Classification

Each incident is coded according to standardized categories:

  • Livestock Raiding: Theft of cattle, goats, camels, etc.
  • Armed Attack: Direct assault on persons or property
  • Ambush: Surprise attacks on travelers or vehicles
  • Abduction: Kidnapping or hostage-taking
  • Displacement: Forced population movement

Verification Process

To ensure data quality and reliability:

  • Cross-referencing multiple sources for each incident
  • Verification of geographic locations using GPS coordinates where available
  • Temporal accuracy checking against published dates
  • Flagging of unconfirmed or disputed reports
  • Regular dataset audits and corrections

Limitations & Caveats

Users should be aware of the following limitations:

  • Media reporting bias toward high-casualty events
  • Underreporting in remote or marginalized areas
  • Delays in incident reporting and documentation
  • Varying levels of detail across sources
  • Potential for double-counting across sources

About the Project

Project Overview

The Kenya Banditry Incident Database is an open-source research initiative dedicated to systematically documenting, analyzing, and disseminating information about banditry incidents in Kenya. By aggregating media reports and official sources, we aim to provide researchers, policymakers, journalists, and the public with reliable, comprehensive data on this critical security challenge.

Banditry—encompassing livestock raiding, armed attacks, and violent conflicts over resources—has profound humanitarian, economic, and political consequences in Kenya's pastoral regions. Yet systematic data collection has been limited, hindering evidence-based responses. This project addresses that gap.

Our goals include: enhancing transparency around security incidents, enabling empirical research on conflict patterns, supporting evidence-based policymaking, and fostering public awareness and accountability.
